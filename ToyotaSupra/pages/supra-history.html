How Toyota Conceived the Supra

The Toyota Supra was conceived as an evolution of the Toyota Celica, a popular sports car in the 1970s. The idea was to create a more powerful and refined version that could compete with the best sports cars of the time. Toyota aimed to achieve a perfect balance between performance, comfort, and reliability, which would set the Supra apart from other sports cars. The result was a vehicle that quickly became an icon in the automotive world.

The Evolution of the Supra: Generations 1 through 5

First Generation (A40/A50, 1978-1981)

The first generation of the Supra, known as the A40/A50, was introduced in 1978. It was essentially a longer, wider, and more powerful version of the Celica. Powered by a 2.6L inline-six engine, the first Supra was designed to be a grand tourer, offering a blend of performance and comfort. Despite its modest beginnings, the first-generation Supra laid the groundwork for the models that followed.

Second Generation (A60, 1981-1986)

The A60 generation, introduced in 1981, marked the Supra's evolution into a more performance-oriented vehicle. With sharper styling, improved handling, and a more powerful 2.8L inline-six engine, the second generation solidified the Supra’s reputation as a capable sports car. The A60 also saw the introduction of advanced technologies such as electronic fuel injection and independent rear suspension.

Third Generation (A70, 1986-1993)

The A70 Supra, introduced in 1986, was a significant leap forward in terms of performance and technology. It was the first Supra to be offered with a turbocharged engine, the legendary 7M-GTE, which produced up to 232 horsepower. The A70 also featured advanced technologies such as ABS, TEMS (Toyota Electronic Modulated Suspension), and a limited-slip differential, making it a true competitor in the sports car market.

Fourth Generation (A80, 1993-2002)

The fourth-generation A80 Supra, introduced in 1993, is arguably the most iconic of all Supras. Powered by the 2JZ-GTE 3.0L inline-six engine, the A80 became a legend in the tuning and racing communities. Its robust engine and rear-wheel-drive layout made it a favorite for modifications, and it became famous worldwide thanks to its appearances in movies and video games. The A80 remains one of the most sought-after Japanese sports cars to this day.

Fifth Generation (A90, 2019-2025)

After a 17-year hiatus, Toyota revived the Supra nameplate with the A90 generation in 2019. Developed in collaboration with BMW, the A90 Supra shares its platform and engine with the BMW Z4. While it faced criticism for not being a purely Toyota design, the A90 has been praised for its performance and driving dynamics. The 3.0L turbocharged inline-six engine, combined with a lightweight chassis and advanced technology, made the A90 a worthy successor to the Supra lineage.

Why the Supra is Being Discontinued in 2025

The decision to discontinue the Supra in 2025 is a reflection of the changing automotive landscape. With increasing regulations on emissions and fuel efficiency, coupled with the shift towards electrification, traditional sports cars like the Supra are becoming harder to justify for manufacturers. Additionally, the collaboration with BMW, which allowed Toyota to bring the Supra back, is coming to an end. While the Supra’s discontinuation is a sad moment for enthusiasts, its legacy as one of the greatest sports cars will endure.
